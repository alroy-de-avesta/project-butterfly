/Introductive Memo

These writings are my notes on the world. Perhaps they would appear incandescent to the average reader and require the heat of devoted study. In any case, let it be known that I was here, so that there might be an analysis in all of these lost memories, those fluttering butterflies of faith that allow us to breathe. If I might gain notice, I would like to cast a light on these colorful flutters, but to do so, I must also risk crushing such beauties, curing the winged pastels under my shoes. Such is the true horrors of recollection, but to me, it is my destiny to do as such, to destroy innate convictions and bask in the light of sorrows!

Thus I declare, let it be known to myself henceforth: that my life would be filled with great turmoil, but it would be more magnificent and more joyful than any man in history! 
---

/Taking Notes

1:  
    Write the name and author of the primary source to take notes on.

2: 
    Do some skimming and research. 
    Type out a personalized table of subjects.

3:  
    Write a broad purpose statement.
        
4: 
    If needed, include a reference delimiter with URL links to images, diagrams, and secondary soures.

5:  
    Take notes on the text. Skip editor/translator notes.
    For each concept you encounter in the text, start a "newline package" with this structure:
    ---
        name of the concept
        > a representation
        : an example
    ---

6: 
    List out additional perspectives external to the primary source and any potential objections.
    ---
        + an additional perspectives
        - an objection 
    ---

7:
    Write a summary of each "section" (chapter, introduction, appendix), covering the main themes and symbols of the text.
